Artificial neural network is a type of machine learning algorithm inspired by the human brain.

Our brains are comprised of a network of biological neurons that fire signals upon receiving stimulus, ultimately telling our bodies what function to perform.

From Biological to Artificial

An artificial neuron models a biological neuron in a simplified manner.
Here, the previous biological nerve system is represented by an artificial neuron with two inputs and a output.

Between two neurons, the biological axon-to-dendrite connection is simplified to an edge, and signals transmitted are represented with numbers.

The input on top is connected to the central neuron with a thick edge, thus the signal is amplified by a factor of 2 when it reaches the neuron.

The input at the bottom, however, has a thinner connection to the neuron, thus the signal is reduced to 2, resulting in a final signal of 2 + 2 = 4 received, instead of 3 + 2 = 5.

The thickness of the edge is called weight in machine learning.

Next, the neuron processes the signal by shaking itself. In this case, doing so incremented the signal by 1.

The “shakiness” of a neuron is called bias, which can both increase or decrease the value of the signal by any set amount.

Finally, an activation function is used to simulate how "fired" the neuron is.

ReLU, a type of activation functions, is used here. It preserves the signal value if it is 0 or greater.

In our case, a positive signal would fully fire the neuron, so the value 5 is preserved and becomes the final output of the neuron.

Apple? Banana?

Using an artificial neuron with two inputs, we can build the simplest neural network: perceptron.

We are going to use it to distinguish between apple and banana. To put it more formally, perceptron can do binary classification.

To simplify the matter, let’s assume that only two characteristics differs between apple and banana: color and shape.

Apple is red and spherical, and banana is yellow and cylindrical.

The perceptron here receives two signals. The input on top receives a "sphere" signal, and the one at the bottom receives a "red" signal.

These signals are sent to the artificial neuron in the center to be processed.

The artificial neuron shakes itself and processes the signal. It then outputs the signal to show the final result: an apple.

Similarly, a combination of "cylinder" and "yellow" signals are being input into the perceptron.

These signals are again sent to the artificial neuron in the center to be processed.

The result of this combination of signal is a banana!
