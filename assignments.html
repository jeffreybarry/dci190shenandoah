Assignments

Understanding technology through analysis and writing

Students are often anxious to dive into learning how to code, but conceptual knowledge forms a long-term foundation.

Each of the assignments enhances your ability to describe the functional aspects of online magazine production. Your understanding of these aspects can be transferred to any type of online site or digital product.

Reflections

For specified weeks you will write a reflection that examines your own thinking about what you're learning in this course. These metacognitive reflections should be between 100 - 200 words. You will have a writing prompt to kickstart your reflection, though feel free to vary your response and address another topic that's puzzling you. Thinking about what you are learning, by expressing those thoughts in your own words, is an excellent learning skill that you should consider applying for many aspects of your life.

The reflections will be graded on a ✓-, ✓, ✓+ scale.

Discussion questions

For specified weeks you will submit, prior to class, two questions that you would like to have discussed in class. These questions should be thoughtful and demonstrate familiarity with the course. Your questions should avoid easy and obvious answers. Instead, you should aim your questions to provoke discussion and even debate among your classmates.

The reflections will be graded on a ✓-, ✓, ✓+ scale.

Teams

Publishing, as with most types of work, is a team effort. For the majority of your work in this course, you will engage in a team initiative that contributes real value to an aspect of publishing Shenandoah.

You'll be given appropriate technical support and knowledge to help guide you through the work of each team. So, don't worry at the beginning of the course if a team's focus is new and unfamiliar to you.

You can choose one of the following teams:

  • documentation
  • multimedia
  • newsletter
  • project management

Keep scrolling to learn more about the duties of each team

Documentation team

The documentation team describes the workflow of publishing Shenandoah.

Deliverables: Clearly written descriptions of each task in preparing an issue. Your knowledge of these tasks is gained through interviewing the staff of Shenandoah.

Multimedia team

The multimedia team will figure out how to handle audio and video elements in Shenandoah.

Deliverables: Prototype the technical means to implement audio and video in Shenandoah. Identify all past uses of audio in earlier online issues of Shenandoah.

Newsletter team

The newsletter team will implement an email newsletter for Shenandoah.

Deliverables: Research different email newsletter providers, e.g., mailchimp. Identify pros, cons, and costs. Recommend a specific provider and work with Shenandoah staff to implement the newsletter.

Project management team

The project management team tracks the progress of each team as well as the production tasks for the upcoming issue of Shenandoah.

Deliverables: Master the use of GitHub as a project management tool and for tracking issues. Use GitHub as the platform for tracking the work of the teams in this course and establish best practices for tracking the workflow of Shenandoah.

Team-led Class Presentation and Discussion

Each team will present their findings to the class and lead a discussion on the topic. The presentation should be approximately 20 minutes followed by approximately 30 minutes of discussion. The presenting team should be prepared with a set of five questions for prompting the discussion.

Team Deliverables

Each team will produce a work product that is a set of documentation on GitHub that describes the work of their team. The documentation team will set guidelines for how the teams should publish their work on GitHub, but each team will have primary responsibility for preparing their own documentation.

Final Reflection

At the end of the term, you will write a personal reflection (at least 500 words) of your experience in this course. Discuss how what you have learned could apply to your future studies at W&L and, more importantly, to your career and life after W&L.

Class participation

This portion of your grade is not simply for showing up (i.e., attendance) but also for contributing to discussions in class. Work is a collaborative task that takes practice. A large part of professional work is communicating with colleagues. The classroom is a good place to practice the type of workplace discussions you will experience in meetings throughout your career.
